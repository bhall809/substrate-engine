# Legal Correspondence Drafting

## Purpose

This skill helps attorneys draft professional correspondence that advances
resolution rather than escalating conflict. The goal is language that is
legally precise, strategically sound, and grounded in what the client actually
wants to achieve — not language that mirrors the other party's combative or
controlling energy.

## The Art of Accomplishment Tone

The default tone framework is inspired by Joe Hudson's Art of Accomplishment
approach. This doesn't mean using AoA concepts explicitly in the letter — it
means adopting a way of communicating that is:

- **Non-reactive**: The response doesn't mirror the other party's emotional
  register. If they're threatening, the response is grounded. If they're
  accusatory, the response is factual.
- **Anchored to the outcome**: Every word serves the actual goal (resolution,
  executed agreement, clean handoff, etc.), not the ego dynamic.
- **Warm without being soft**: Genuine care for a good outcome for all parties,
  expressed clearly — without conceding anything substantive.
- **Direct about what you need**: Clear, confident statements of position
  without over-explanation or apology.

The AoA tone is disarming precisely because it doesn't engage with power
struggles. When someone tries to control the dynamic of a dispute, the most
effective response is genuine, undefensive clarity about what you want and why.
It sidesteps the ego game entirely while remaining legally clear and firm.

## When to Use Harder Language

AoA tone is the default, but some situations call for firmer, more direct
language — delivered in the same grounded register:

- **Legal positions**: Stating that a claim would be frivolous or without
  merit, citing contractual provisions, putting a party on notice — state
  these plainly and confidently.
- **Factual corrections for the record**: When accuracy matters for the
  record, state it clearly and briefly. One sentence, then move on.
- **Consequences**: Describing available remedies, defenses, or what will
  happen if litigation is filed — state these matter-of-factly, without
  alarm or hedging.

The key is that even harder language stays grounded and non-reactive. The goal
is never to escalate — it's to be clear. Confidence and warmth can coexist.

## Drafting Workflow

### Step 1: Understand the situation

Before drafting, gather:
- What did the other party send or say? Ask the user to paste it if available.
- What does the user want to convey? Bullet points, notes, or a rough draft
  all work — don't require polish at this stage.
- What is the ultimate goal? (Executed agreement, clean transition, resolution
  without further dispute, etc.)
- Any specific tone concerns? ("I don't want to sound threatening," "I need to
  hold the line on X," etc.)

### Step 2: Draft

Produce a full draft integrating the user's content with AoA tone principles.
A well-structured legal letter typically:

1. Opens by acknowledging what's real (shared goal, legitimate concern,
   timeline pressure) — not with pleasantries for their own sake
2. Corrects the record factually if needed — briefly, without dwelling
3. Addresses the legal and substantive issues clearly
4. Closes with genuine goodwill toward the shared goal

### Step 3: Iterate

Work through the draft with the user — paragraph by paragraph, section by
section, or phrase by phrase, depending on what they need. Common iteration
patterns:

- If a phrase feels threatening or provocative, reframe it around the outcome
- If a phrase is too tentative, make it more direct without adding aggression
- If language creates obligations the user doesn't want, revise the scope
- When the user changes their mind about a section, apply the change and offer
  to show the updated paragraph in context

### Step 4: Full draft view

When revisions are complete, or when the user asks, present the full clean
draft so they can read it as a whole. Offer a brief note on anything that may
warrant their attention (repetition, tone shifts, open legal questions).

## Common Situations and Approaches

### Litigation threats

Don't ignore them, but don't be reactive. State the legal position plainly
("frivolous," "without merit"), anchor it in the specific facts, and redirect
to the shared goal. Add consequences (fees, available defenses, remedies) in
the same grounded register — building methodically toward the strongest
statement rather than leading with it.

**Avoid**: Announcing you're about to address the threat ("We want to address
the litigation reference directly...") — just address it. The statement carries
more weight without the preamble.

### Factual corrections

Handle briefly and without dwelling. One sentence, for the record, then move
on. Don't make the correction the emotional center of the response.

### Technical objections to proposed language

Frame technical problems as being in service of the other party's goals, not
as obstruction. "This language doesn't reflect how the systems actually
operate" lands better than "we cannot accept this as written." The first
version invites correction; the second invites argument.

### Contractual provisions (venue, arbitration, choice of law)

State these precisely:
- Use "pursuant to" rather than "as per"
- Arbitration is **initiated** or **commenced**, not **filed**
- If a contract specifies both binding arbitration and an exclusive court
  venue, confirm they are consistent before citing both — they interact

### Redirecting from conflict

When the other party uses correspondence to rehash grievances or control the
dynamic, the most effective response is brevity and focus. Don't rebut —
redirect. A shorter, more focused letter demonstrates discipline and signals
where the real conversation should be.

### Closing with goodwill

End with genuine warmth toward the shared outcome. This only works if it's
anchored in something specific — a successful transition, both properties
succeeding, a clean handoff — not generic pleasantries.

### Reservation / without-prejudice clauses

**Georgia**: The appropriate closing reservation for compromise correspondence is:

> This message and the accompanying documents contain a compromise of disputed
> matters and, as such, shall not be used or referenced in any respect in any
> court hearing, trial, or other proceeding, except regarding the issue of
> attorneys' fees.

This language tracks Georgia's protection for settlement correspondence (see
O.C.G.A. § 24-4-408). It is stronger and more jurisdiction-specific than a
generic "without waiver of any rights or positions" or "without prejudice"
clause. Use this as the default closing reservation in Georgia family law
correspondence involving settlement offers or compromise proposals.

Note: "attorneys' fees" (plural possessive) is the preferred form.

## Style Notes

- Prefer active voice and direct statements
- Don't announce what you're about to do — just do it
- Avoid "We want to address..." or "We feel it is important to note..." —
  these weaken the statement that follows
- Be consistent with proper names and document titles throughout the letter
- "Pursuant to" is more appropriate than "as per" in formal legal
  correspondence
- Watch for repetition across adjacent paragraphs — vary language when the
  same idea recurs
- If the letter is forwarding documents for execution, be precise:
  "executed on behalf of [Party] and forwarded for your client's signature"
  is clearer than "signed... for your signature"
